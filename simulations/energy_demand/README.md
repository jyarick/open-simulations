# Interactive Energy Demand Modeling Tool

An interactive modeling tool used in the **Powering the Community (PTC)** AI competition hosted through the **EQuIPD Grant at the University of Florida**.

This notebook allows students to explore how different types of buildings contribute to electricity demand at the **city/grid level**, using real energy data and interactive transformations.

---

## Overview

Electric power systems must be designed around **peak demand**, not just total energy use.  
However, peaks often emerge from the **interaction of many different users** rather than from a single large consumer.

This tool helps students explore those interactions by allowing them to:

- Build a model of a community’s electricity demand
- Combine multiple building types (hospital, mall, school, etc.)
- Modify demand profiles through controlled transformations
- Observe how individual users contribute to **grid-level peak load**

The goal is to help students understand **what actually drives peak demand on the grid**.

---

## What the Tool Does

The notebook loads **building energy demand profiles derived from the NREL ComStock™ dataset** and converts them into a consistent modeling framework.

Students can:

- Select **10 building user types** representing a city
- Rename or duplicate users to represent multiple buildings
- Apply transformations to demand profiles (scale, shift, smoothing, noise)
- Combine users into a **Total Grid Demand Profile**
- Analyze:

  - peak demand
  - timing of peaks
  - user contributions to grid stress

The result is an interactive environment for experimenting with **how cities consume electricity over time**.

---

## Key Concepts Explored

- Energy vs. power in time-series data
- Peak demand and grid stress
- Coincidence and synchronization of loads
- Effects of user composition on grid behavior
- Scenario exploration through controlled transformations

---

## Machine Learning Extension (Optional)

The notebook itself uses **explicit calculations**, which are preferred when analyzing individual scenarios.

However, the structured scenarios generated by this tool can also be used to train **machine learning models** that approximate the system behavior.

Machine learning becomes useful when:

- exploring **very large scenario spaces**
- predicting **full demand curves**
- performing **inverse design** (e.g., reducing peak demand through changes in user composition)

Machine learning is introduced here **not to replace physical calculations, but to approximate and invert them when the design space becomes too large to explore directly**.

## Educational Use

This tool was developed for the **Powering the Community (PTC) AI Competition**, where student teams map the energy demand of their community based on major power users within a **10-mile radius of their school**.

The activity helps students move from thinking about **individual buildings** to understanding **system-level grid behavior**.

## Data Source

Energy demand profiles used in this tool are derived from the **NREL ComStock™ dataset**, developed by the **National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL)**.

ComStock provides high-resolution simulated energy consumption data for commercial buildings across the United States and is widely used for building energy analysis and grid modeling.

More information about the dataset can be found at:

https://comstock.nrel.gov/
